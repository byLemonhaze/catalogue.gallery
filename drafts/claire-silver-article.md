# Taste Is the New Skill: Claire Silver and the Aesthetics of Co-Agency

**Abstract:**

In the noisy discourse around AI art, most arguments still rely on a tired binary: either the machine is a threat to art, or it is a novelty engine. [Claire Silver](/artist/clairesilver) rejects both positions. Across her public writing, interviews, and exhibition history, she defines practice not as prompt-output spectacle but as sustained collaboration with emergent systems. This article examines Silver’s work through a theory of co-agency: a model in which meaning is produced neither by human intention alone nor model computation alone, but by iterative negotiation between the two. In that framework, technical execution is de-centered, while discernment, curation, and conceptual direction become primary artistic acts.

## Beyond the Skill Barrier

Silver’s most repeated provocation, “taste is the new skill,” is often misunderstood as anti-craft. It is better read as a historical argument: image culture has moved from scarcity of production toward scarcity of judgment. If software can render near-infinite visual possibilities, then the artist’s differentiator becomes selection, synthesis, and refusal.

This position is consistent across her public statements. On her own site, Silver frames AI as a collaborator and describes an image-making process that moves through generation, inpainting, and compositional intervention rather than one-shot automation. The work is not a button press; it is an editorial practice under conditions of overwhelming abundance.

## Co-Agency as Method

Christie’s description of Silver emphasizes an “ongoing visual conversation with AI.” That phrase is useful because conversation implies reciprocity, revision, and friction. Silver herself has stated that neither she alone nor a model alone could produce the same outcomes; the work exists in the interaction.

Seen this way, authorship in Silver’s practice is procedural. It appears in the design of constraints, the direction of iteration, and the final cut through thousands of machine-legible options. The artist’s voice is not erased by AI; it is distributed across stages of prompting, rejection, compositing, and post-processing, then recomposed as a coherent aesthetic position.

## Thematic Program: Innocence, Trauma, Divinity

Silver’s declared thematic terrain includes innocence, trauma, social hierarchy, disability, and divinity, often staged against a transhumanist horizon. What distinguishes this program is not only subject matter but tonality: the images oscillate between intimacy and myth, personal wound and archetypal narrative.

In this sense, Silver’s project belongs to a longer history of artists using new tools to revisit old symbolic structures. The “hero’s journey” and religious iconography in her corpus are not nostalgic returns; they are stress tests for how spiritual and psychological motifs survive when generated in partnership with nonhuman systems.

## From Native Internet Practice to Institutional Validation

Silver’s trajectory also marks an institutional shift in how AI art is received. Her work has circulated in crypto-native contexts and major auction ecosystems, including projects presented by Christie’s and Sotheby’s, while her profile is associated with museum-level collection discourse. This movement matters: it indicates that AI collaboration has moved from platform subculture into art-historical adjudication.

A key example is Christie’s AI-focused sales programming, where Silver’s work appeared as part of a broader attempt to contextualize machine-assisted practice for traditional collectors. In those settings, the central question is no longer whether AI art is “real,” but which artists are building durable visual languages rather than chasing technical novelty.

## Applied Practice: The Gucci Experiment

Silver’s collaboration with Gucci (with Emi Kusano) offers a practical view of her method outside gallery framing. In Gucci’s own account of the project, the process moved from written narratives and poem-like prompts to generated imagery, then through hand refinement, sculptural interpretation, and textile translation.

This pipeline is significant because it demonstrates that Silver’s practice can survive medium transfer. The same co-agential logic that structures her digital works can be re-instantiated in fashion, material design, and physically produced objects. AI, in this model, is not a genre. It is an artistic operating system.

## Conclusion: Post-Tool, Pro-Voice

Claire Silver’s importance is not that she “uses AI.” Thousands of artists now do. Her importance is that she has articulated a coherent philosophy for why and how to use it without surrendering authorship. She reframes artistic labor around intent, taste, and iterative discipline at a moment when technical access is rapidly democratizing.

If the 2010s argued about whether digital images could be owned, the 2020s are arguing about whether machine-mediated images can still be deeply human. Silver’s answer is structurally clear: humanity does not disappear in collaboration with systems; it becomes visible in the decisions that systems cannot make for us.
